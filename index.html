Where Longevity Meets the Brain: Aging Suppressors and Sex Chromosomes in the Mouse Brain Atlas

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
klotho aging suppressors X chromosome choroid plexus Allen Brain Atlas Python

The Dubal lab investigates the biology of longevity from two complementary angles: the role of aging-suppressor proteins like klotho and FGF21 in protecting the brain, and the role of sex chromosome dosage in determining why females tend to live longer and are more resilient to certain brain diseases. I was curious how these two threads intersect at the transcriptional level — specifically, where in the brain are the aging suppressors expressed, and how broadly are X-chromosome escapee genes (which are present at double dosage in XX individuals) distributed across brain structures?

To explore this, I pulled in situ hybridization expression data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas for eight aging suppressor / longevity genes and four X-chromosome escapee genes across eight brain regions — including the choroid plexus, which the Dubal lab's work has highlighted as a critical secretory hub for longevity factors into CSF.

What this is. A personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. All data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas public API (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004). ISH expression energy is a bulk regional metric (sum of pixel intensity × density, normalized by structure volume). Analysis and interpretation are mine alone.

Part 1 — Aging Suppressor Genes

Klotho (Kl) peaks in choroid plexus and cerebellum — the two known expression sites in brain. Expression energy is 3.49 in choroid plexus and 3.61 in cerebellum (Purkinje cells), both substantially higher than hippocampus (2.97) or isocortex (1.32). Fgf21 (0.66) and Igf1 (1.22) also peak in choroid plexus, marking this structure as the brain's primary secretory hub for CSF-borne longevity factors.

Several patterns are biologically interpretable:

  • The choroid plexus concentrates secreted longevity factors (Kl, Fgf21, Igf1, Sirt1). This is consistent with their role as CSF-secreted proteins that act on downstream brain circuits. The Dubal lab's finding that peripheral or CSF klotho elevation enhances hippocampal synaptic plasticity fits perfectly: the choroid plexus is the bottleneck through which these factors enter the brain, making it a compelling therapeutic target for cognitive aging.
  • Gdf11 and Gdf15 show distinct distributions — Gdf11 peaks in thalamus and olfactory bulb, Gdf15 in olfactory bulb, both relatively low in hippocampus. If "young blood" factors like GDF11 act on the brain, these expression patterns suggest they do so via circuits beyond the canonical hippocampal plasticity targets.
  • Tert (telomerase reverse transcriptase) is expressed in isocortex and hippocampus at moderate levels, consistent with neural progenitor activity and the known role of telomere maintenance in brain aging. Its relatively uniform distribution contrasts with the choroid-plexus-biased secreted factors.

Part 2 — X-Chromosome Escapee Genes

Several X-escapee genes show robust expression throughout the brain:

  • Kdm5c (KDM5C / JARID1C, H3K4me3 demethylase) is the most broadly and highly expressed of the group — expression energy 6.98 in isocortex, 6.85 in olfactory bulb, 4.65 in hippocampus. This chromatin regulator shapes the active promoter landscape genome-wide. In XX individuals, double dosage of Kdm5c could systematically maintain a more youthful chromatin state across brain cell types.
  • Usp9x (ubiquitin protease, X-linked, escapee) is broadly expressed across all regions (2.3–6.0 expression energy) and is required for synaptic protein stability. Dosage differences in Usp9x between XX and XY brains could influence the rate of protein aggregation and clearance in aging neurons.
  • Kdm6a (UTX, H3K27me3 demethylase) peaks in choroid plexus (5.88) and olfactory bulb (4.54) — the same structures that are hubs for aging suppressor expression. The convergence of Kdm6a and klotho in choroid plexus is intriguing: UTX-mediated chromatin opening could regulate klotho transcription itself, linking the sex chromosome dosage and longevity-factor axes.

Why this connects to the lab's open questions

  • "How do sex chromosomes impact longevity and disease susceptibility?" — The X-escapee gene expression map provides a starting point for asking which brain structures are most influenced by sex chromosome dosage. KDM6A in choroid plexus, and KDM5C broadly across cortex and hippocampus, are positioned to exert systematic effects on chromatin state and gene regulation that could differ between XX and XY brains.
  • "How do aging-suppressors impact the brain?" — The choroid plexus concentration of Kl, Fgf21, and Igf1 frames this structure as the primary node for aging-suppressor input to the brain. Understanding what regulates their expression in choroid plexus epithelial cells — and how that changes with aging or disease — seems like a high-leverage question.
  • "Can anti-aging strategies prevent cognitive decline?" — The hippocampal expression of klotho and the X-escapee chromatin regulators suggests that both longevity-factor delivery (via CSF from choroid plexus) and chromatin state maintenance (via KDM6A/KDM5C dosage) converge on the hippocampus — the canonical structure for cognitive aging. These two axes could potentially be targeted in combination.

Methods
